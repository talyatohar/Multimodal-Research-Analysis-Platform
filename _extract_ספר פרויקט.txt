Determining the neural circuits supporting reading in children with left vs right amblyopia("lazy eye”) using EEGIntroductionAmblyopia, commonly referred to as “lazy eye,” is a neurodevelopmental visual disorder characterized by reduced visual acuity that cannot be explained by structural abnormalities of the eye alone. The condition typically develops during early childhood as a result of abnormal visual experience, including strabismus, refractive imbalance, or visual deprivation. Beyond reduced acuity, amblyopia has been associated with deficits in binocular vision, oculomotor control, visual attention, and higher-order visual processing. Recent evidence suggests that amblyopia may also influence reading fluency and audiovisual integration processes. Efficient reading relies on accurate and temporally synchronized visual and auditory processing, requiring coordination between visual perception, eye movements, attention, executive functions, and language-related neural networks. Children with amblyopia often demonstrate slower reading speed and altered visual scanning patterns, potentially reflecting differences in the neural mechanisms supporting reading acquisition and fluency. In particular, differences between left-eye and right-eye amblyopia may be relevant for understanding reading-related neural processing. Since reading depends on lateralized visual and language networks, asymmetric visual input may influence how information is integrated across hemispheres during reading tasks. Previous studies have suggested that amblyopia may affect functional connectivity, attentional engagement, and audiovisual synchronization, highlighting the importance of investigating neural activity alongside behavioral performance. The current project focuses on the development of an integrated experimental and engineering platform designed to synchronize behavioral, physiological, and neurophysiological measurements during reading tasks. The system combines EEG, eye-tracking, and heart-rate acquisition with controlled visual and auditory experimental paradigms in order to investigate reading fluency, visual processing, and audiovisual integration in children with amblyopia.This project book serves both as research documentation and as a technical system-design reference. In addition to describing the scientific rationale and experimental structure, it documents the architecture of the experimental interface, synchronization logic, signal acquisition pipelines, preprocessing methods, and analytical components used throughout the project.Experimental Procedure The experimental session begins with the participant seated in front of a computer screen. The child is connected to a 64-channel EEG system, a Corsano wrist device for heart-rate and HRV recording, and a Tobii X3-120 eye-tracking system. After EEG and physiological sensors are connected, the eye-tracker calibration procedure is performed to ensure accurate gaze recording throughout the reading tasks.1. Oral Reading - Erased Textparticipants read the text aloud while letters gradually disappear from the screen over time. This condition combines active vocal reading with dynamic visual pacing.2. Silent Reading - Erased Text participants read the text silently while letters gradually disappear from the screen. No auditory pacing cues are presented during this task.3. Silent Reading - Erased Text with Metronomeparticipants read silently while letters gradually disappear from the screen and rhythmic metronome clicks are simultaneously presented in the background. This condition is designed to examine the interaction between visual pacing and external auditory timing cues.4. Silent Reading - Static Text with Metronomeparticipants read silently while the full text remains continuously visible on the screen. Rhythmic metronome clicks are presented throughout the task.5. Silent Reading - Static Text participants read silently while the full text remains continuously visible on the screen. 6. Resting stateIn addition to the reading tasks, the experiment includes a two-minute resting-state baseline condition, during which the child looks at the screen without performing an active task. This baseline is used to characterize resting neural and physiological activity and to provide a comparison point for task-related EEG, eye-tracking, and heart-rate measures.After each reading task, the child answers multiple-choice comprehension questions about the text. These questions are used to verify reading engagement and to provide behavioral measures of comprehension for each experimental condition.Experimental System StructureThe experimental paradigm is controlled using multiple synchronized acquisition systems distributed across separate computers.The reading tasks are presented using E-Prime, which runs on the participant-facing laptop. This computer is also used for the eye-tracking system, including both the Tobii X3-120 device and the Tobii Pro Lab software used for gaze recording and data acquisition. Since the eye-tracking system must remain directly synchronized with stimulus presentation, E-Prime and Tobii Pro Lab are operated on the same computer.EEG acquisition is performed on a separate recording computer connected to the 64-channel EEG system. The reading tasks used in the current experiment do not require direct event-trigger synchronization between E-Prime and the EEG acquisition computer, allowing the EEG recording system to operate independently during the tasks.Physiological heart-rate and HRV measurements are collected using the Corsano wearable device. Data from the Corsano device are recorded directly through the Corsano online platform and therefore do not depend on a dedicated local acquisition computer.This distributed system architecture enables simultaneous acquisition of behavioral, gaze, neural, and physiological data during the experimental session while maintaining separation between the different recording systems.Eye-Tracking Database: Relevant FieldsExport a file from the software "Tobii pro lab" using the following format:1.
